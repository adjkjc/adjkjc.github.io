Etymology[edit]

From Middle English -nes, -nesse, from Old English -nis, -nes, from Proto-Germanic *-nassuz. This suffix was formed already in Proto-Germanic by false division of the final consonant *-n- of the preceding stem + the actual suffix *-assuz. The latter was in turn derived from an earlier *-at(s)-tuz, from the verbal suffix *-at-janƒÖ + the noun suffix *-√æuz.

Suffix[edit]

-ness

  1. Appended to adjectives to form nouns meaning "the state of being (the adjective)", "the quality of being (the adjective)", or "the measure of being (the adjective)".
    ‎calm + ‎-ness ‚Üí ‎calmness
    ‎dark + ‎-ness ‚Üí ‎darkness
    ‎kind + ‎-ness ‚Üí ‎kindness
    ‎one + ‎-ness ‚Üí ‎oneness
  2. Appended to words of other parts of speech to form nouns (often nonce words or terms in philosophy) meaning the state/quality/measure of the idea represented by these words.
    ‎that + ‎-ness ‚Üí ‎thatness
    ‎tree + ‎-ness ‚Üí ‎treeness
    ‎thug + ‎-ness ‚Üí ‎thugness

Usage notes[edit]

  • If an adjective ends in -y, then this changes to -i- when -ness is suffixed. This occurs both when the -y is the suffix -y (‚Äúhaving the quality of‚Äù), as in mess ‚Üí messy ‚Üí messiness (hence -y ‚Üí -i-), but also in other cases, as in comely ‚Üí comeliness. It does not, however, occur when the -y is part of the root, as in spry ‚Üí spryness.
  • Plurals are formed by adding -es, e.g. happiness ‚Üí happinesses.

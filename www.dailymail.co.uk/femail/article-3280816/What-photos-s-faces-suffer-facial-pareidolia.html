We have all looked up at the sky and seen a little face in the clouds smiling back, but when you glance at these pictures what do you see?

Is this a leaf with caterpillar marks, a pear full of blemishes, the back of an alarm clock, a plug and other household objects - or have you spotted a host of little faces smiling, grimacing and laughing back to you?

If it's the latter, these images are causing you to experience facial pareidolia, the psychological phenomenon of seeing faces in inanimate objects.

Professor Kang Lee. from the University of Toronto who carried out a study on facial pareidolia, explains: 'Most people think you have to be mentally abnormal to see these types of images, so individuals reporting this phenomenon are often ridiculed.

But our findings suggest its common for people to see non-existent features because human brains are uniquely wired to recognise faces, so that even when theres only a slight suggestion of facial features the brain automatically interprets it as a face.'

Lee adds: The tendency to detect faces in ambiguous visual information is perhaps highly adaptive given the supreme importance of faces in our social life and the high cost resulting from failure to detect a true face, explains Lee.

After analysing the brain's response to seeing faces in inanimate objects the researchers, from the University of Toronto, Beijing Jiaotong University, Xidian University, and the Institute of Automation Chinese Academy of Sciences, discovered that the recognition occurs in the frontal and visual cortex.

The frontal cortex in each participants brain sent signals to the posterior visual cortex, which then enhanced the recognisable elements to try and interpret the stimulus.
